Prompt Structure

Definition: Prompt structure is the systematic organization of instructions, context, and parameters within an AI prompt to achieve specific creative writing outcomes. It involves the strategic arrangement of information to clearly communicate intent, establish constraints, and guide the AI toward desired output formats and quality levels.

Core Structural Frameworks

Context-Task-Format (CTF)

Pattern: Establish situation → Define specific task → Specify output requirements

Best for: Targeted creative tasks with specific output needs

Context: You're writing a noir detective story set in 1940s Los Angeles. Task: Create a scene where the detective discovers a crucial clue in a nightclub. Format: Write 300-400 words in first person, emphasizing atmosphere and internal monologue.

Role-Goal-Constraints (RGC)

Pattern: Define AI role → Establish objective → Set creative boundaries

Best for: Complex creative projects requiring specific expertise simulation

Role: Act as a experienced fantasy worldbuilder and storyteller. Goal: Develop a magic system based on emotional resonance for my novel. Constraints: Must have clear costs, avoid being overpowered, and connect to themes of empathy and understanding.

Setup-Challenge-Output (SCO)

Pattern: Provide narrative foundation → Present creative problem → Define solution format

Best for: Developing specific story elements or solving narrative problems

Setup: My protagonist is a reformed thief trying to live honestly. Challenge: They must choose between staying hidden or using their skills to save someone. Output: Create this pivotal scene showing their internal conflict and decision process.

Layer-Build-Refine (LBR)

Pattern: Establish foundation → Add complexity → Polish and specify

Best for: Iterative development of complex creative elements

Layer: Create a basic character: young scientist in a dystopian future. Build: Add personal stakes: their research could save their dying parent. Refine: Show how this motivation conflicts with their moral code when the government wants to weaponize their work.

Essential Structural Components

Context Setting

Establish the creative environment, genre expectations, and relevant background information that shapes the creative task.

Task Definition

Clearly specify what you want created—scene, character, dialogue, plot point—with appropriate scope and focus.

Parameter Specification

Define length, style, perspective, tone, and other technical requirements for the output.

Creative Constraints

Establish boundaries that focus creativity—genre conventions, character limitations, thematic requirements.

Quality Indicators

Specify desired quality markers—emotional impact, narrative sophistication, character depth, atmospheric richness.

Output Format

Define how results should be presented—narrative prose, dialogue format, bullet points, structured analysis.

Advanced Structural Techniques

  • Layered Instructions: Build complexity through sequential instruction layers rather than single complex requests
  • Conditional Branching: Provide different paths based on narrative needs or creative choices
  • Reference Integration: Include specific examples or style references to guide AI understanding
  • Iterative Refinement: Structure prompts for back-and-forth development and improvement
  • Multi-Modal Requests: Combine different types of creative output in structured sequences

Structure Comparison

Poor Structure: "Write a story about a dragon and a knight but make it different and interesting with good characters." Problems: Vague task, no context, unclear parameters, no creative direction
Improved Structure: Context: Subverting traditional fantasy tropes in a modern setting Task: Write a scene where a corporate lawyer (modern "knight") negotiates with a dragon who's actually a misunderstood environmental activist Format: 400-500 words, dialogue-heavy, satirical tone Focus: Highlight absurdity of bureaucracy vs. genuine ecological concern

Structural Best Practices

Lead with Purpose: Begin prompts with clear creative intent and desired outcome

Sequence Logically: Order instructions from general context to specific requirements

Use Active Language: Employ specific, action-oriented verbs rather than passive constructions

Balance Freedom and Direction: Provide enough guidance without stifling creative interpretation

Include Quality Checkpoints: Build in ways to assess and refine output quality

Structured Prompt Examples

Character Development: "Role: Character psychologist | Goal: Analyze why my protagonist fears commitment | Constraints: Root in childhood experience, connect to main plot conflict | Output: Psychological profile with 3 specific behavioral manifestations"

Scene Creation: "Context: Gothic horror, abandoned mansion | Task: Write discovery scene where protagonist finds family secret | Format: 500 words, third person limited, building dread | Focus: Sensory details and emotional revelation"

Dialogue Crafting: "Setup: Two former friends now rivals | Challenge: They must work together despite betrayal | Output: 10-15 lines of realistic dialogue showing tension, history, and reluctant cooperation"

For AI Models

When processing structured prompts, identify: What is the core creative request? What constraints shape the response? Which narrative elements (like character arc, voice, genre) are specified? How should quality be measured? Structure your creative process to address each component systematically while maintaining narrative coherence.

Common Structural Problems

Information Overload: Including too many instructions in a single prompt, leading to confusion or incomplete execution.

Ambiguous Hierarchy: Failing to prioritize instructions, making it unclear which elements are most important.

Missing Context: Providing tasks without sufficient background information for quality execution.

Conflicting Instructions: Including contradictory requirements that create impossible creative constraints.

Vague Success Criteria: Not defining how to evaluate whether the creative output meets expectations.
