Dialogue

Definition: Dialogue is the spoken conversation between characters in a story. Effective dialogue serves multiple purposes: revealing character personality and relationships, advancing the plot, conveying information naturally, and creating realistic human interaction that engages readers.

Functions of Dialogue

Character Revelation

Purpose: Shows personality, background, education, and emotional state through speech patterns

Techniques: Vocabulary choice, sentence structure, dialect, speech rhythms, what's left unsaid

"I reckon we might could try that" vs. "Perhaps we should consider that option" - reveals social background and education

Plot Advancement

Purpose: Moves the story forward through character decisions, revelations, and actions

Techniques: Information sharing, conflict escalation, decision-making, plan formation

"The bridge is out." "Then we go through the forest." - creates new plot direction naturally

Subtext and Tension

Purpose: Conveys underlying meanings, hidden emotions, and unspoken conflicts

Techniques: What characters don't say, double meanings, evasion, implications

"How was your meeting with Sarah?" "Fine." - the brevity suggests something more complex

Exposition and Information

Purpose: Conveys necessary background information and world-building details naturally

Techniques: Questions and answers, casual mentions, explanations that feel organic

"Since the war ended, nothing's been the same" - establishes historical context naturally

Dialogue Techniques

  • Distinct Voice: Each character should sound unique and recognizable
  • Realistic but Focused: More purposeful than real conversation, but still natural
  • Action and Reaction: Characters respond authentically to what others say
  • Interruption and Overlap: Real conversations aren't perfectly orderly
  • Silence and Pauses: What's not said can be as important as what is

Literary Examples Analysis

Ernest Hemingway ("Hills Like White Elephants"): Entire story told through dialogue with heavy subtext about difficult decisions

Jane Austen (Pride and Prejudice): Witty dialogue reveals character intelligence and social dynamics

Elmore Leonard (crime novels): Authentic dialogue that captures distinct character voices and regional speech patterns

AI Prompt Examples

Character-Revealing: "Write dialogue between a nervous teenager and a confident adult, showing their personalities through speech patterns alone."

Subtext-Heavy: "Create a conversation between ex-lovers meeting accidentally, where neither mentions their past relationship directly."

Exposition: "Write dialogue where characters naturally reveal that their city is under magical protection, without it feeling like an info-dump."

Conflict: "Create an argument between siblings that reveals their different values and childhood experiences."

For AI Models

When writing dialogue: Give each character a distinct voice based on their background, personality, and emotional state. Avoid exposition-heavy dialogue that sounds unnatural. Use dialogue to show character growth over time. Remember that conflict in dialogue doesn't always mean arguing - it can be subtle disagreement, misunderstanding, or competing goals. Consider how point of view affects dialogue presentation.

Common Dialogue Mistakes

Info-dumping: Using dialogue solely to convey background information unnaturally.

All characters sound the same: Lack of distinct voice for different characters.

Too formal or stilted: Dialogue that doesn't sound like real speech.

Overuse of names: People don't constantly say each other's names in conversation.

Ignoring subtext: Characters always saying exactly what they mean.

Relationship to Other Concepts

Expresses: Voice - Each character's unique voice emerges through dialogue

Reveals: Character Arc - Character growth manifests in changing speech patterns

Advances: Conflict - Dialogue often escalates or resolves conflicts

Shaped by: Point of View - POV affects how dialogue is presented and filtered

Influenced by: Setting - Time period and location affect speech patterns and vocabulary

Writing Techniques

Read aloud: Dialogue should sound natural when spoken.

Use dialogue tags sparingly: Let the dialogue itself indicate who's speaking when possible.

Show through speech: Let characters reveal themselves through what and how they speak.

Create rhythm: Vary sentence length and structure for natural flow.

Leave space for interpretation: Not everything needs to be explicitly stated.
