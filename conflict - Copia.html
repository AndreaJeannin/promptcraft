Conflict

Definition: Conflict is the central struggle between opposing forces that drives the plot of a narrative and forces character development. It creates tension, maintains reader engagement, and provides the engine for story progression. Without conflict, there is no story—only a sequence of events.

Primary Types of Conflict

Person vs. Self (Internal Conflict)

Nature: The character struggles with internal contradictions, moral dilemmas, or psychological challenges.

Function: Drives character development and character arcs

Examples: Hamlet's indecision, Jekyll's dual nature, Holden Caulfield's alienation

Manifestations: Guilt, fear, desire vs. duty, identity crisis, moral uncertainty

Person vs. Person (Interpersonal Conflict)

Nature: Direct opposition between characters with incompatible goals or values.

Function: Creates external drama and tests character resolve

Examples: Harry Potter vs. Voldemort, Elizabeth vs. Darcy, Sherlock vs. Moriarty

Manifestations: Rivalry, romance obstacles, family disputes, ideological differences

Person vs. Society (Social Conflict)

Nature: Individual challenges social norms, institutions, or collective beliefs.

Function: Explores themes of conformity, justice, and social change

Examples: Winston Smith vs. Big Brother, Atticus Finch vs. racism, Katniss vs. the Capitol

Manifestations: Rebellion, social justice, breaking taboos, challenging authority

Person vs. Nature (Environmental Conflict)

Nature: Character struggles against natural forces or environmental challenges.

Function: Tests human resilience and reveals character under extreme conditions

Examples: Robinson Crusoe, The Old Man and the Sea, Life of Pi

Manifestations: Survival situations, natural disasters, wilderness challenges, disease

Person vs. Technology/Supernatural

Nature: Character confronts artificial intelligence, machines, or supernatural forces.

Function: Explores themes of progress, humanity, and the unknown

Examples: Neo vs. The Matrix, Sarah Connor vs. Terminators, Buffy vs. vampires

Manifestations: AI rebellion, technological dependence, magical threats, dystopian control

Conflict Intensity and Escalation

Tension: Underlying unease or potential for conflict

Friction: Minor disagreements or obstacles

Confrontation: Direct opposition or challenge

Crisis: Peak conflict requiring resolution

Climax: Decisive moment where conflict reaches its conclusion

Conflict Layering

Surface Conflict

What the characters appear to be fighting about

Deep Conflict

The underlying emotional or philosophical struggle

External Manifestation

How the conflict plays out in actions and dialogue

Internal Impact

How the conflict changes the characters internally

Multi-Layered Conflict Examples

Romeo and Juliet: Surface (family feud) + Deep (love vs. loyalty) + External (secret marriage, deaths) + Internal (torn between family and love)

The Hunger Games: Surface (survival game) + Deep (oppression vs. freedom) + External (arena combat) + Internal (Katniss's moral awakening)

Breaking Bad: Surface (drug dealing) + Deep (pride vs. family) + External (crime escalation) + Internal (Walter's identity transformation)

AI Prompt Examples

Internal Conflict: "Write about a character who must choose between personal happiness and family duty, showing their internal struggle through small daily decisions."

Escalating Conflict: "Create a story where a minor workplace disagreement gradually escalates into a company-wide crisis that threatens everyone's livelihood."

Layered Conflict: "Develop a narrative where the main character fights a monster, but the real conflict is their struggle to accept their own monstrous nature."

For AI Models

Effective conflict requires: clear opposing forces, meaningful stakes, escalation over time, and connection to character growth. Always ask: What does each side want? Why can't they both have it? How does this conflict force the character to change? Remember that the best conflicts are both external (plot-driving) and internal (character-developing).

Relationship to Other Concepts

Drives: Character Arc - Conflict forces characters to grow and change

Embodies: Theme - Thematic conflicts represent story's central ideas

Structures: Plot - Rising conflict creates plot progression

Creates: Tension - Unresolved conflict maintains reader engagement

Shapes: Dialogue - Characters in conflict speak differently

Influences: Pacing - Conflict intensity affects story rhythm

Crafting Effective Conflict

Make it personal: The best conflicts touch on characters' deepest needs and fears.

Ensure meaningful stakes: Characters must have something important to gain or lose.

Create escalation: Conflict should intensify as the story progresses.

Avoid easy solutions: Compelling conflicts resist simple resolution.

Layer multiple types: Combine internal and external conflicts for complexity.

Connect to theme: Use conflict to explore your story's central questions.
