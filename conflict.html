Conflict

Definition: Conflict is the central struggle, tension, or opposition that drives a narrative forward and creates dramatic interest. It represents the forces that prevent characters from easily achieving their goals, generating the friction necessary for compelling storytelling.

Types of Conflict

Person vs Person (External)

Nature: Direct opposition between characters with conflicting goals, values, or desires.

Function: Creates immediate dramatic tension and clear stakes through interpersonal struggle.

Examples: Harry Potter vs Voldemort, Sherlock Holmes vs Moriarty, Elizabeth Bennet vs Mr. Darcy (initially)

Key Elements: Opposing motivations, clear antagonist, external obstacles, interpersonal dynamics

Person vs Self (Internal)

Nature: Internal struggle within a character's mind, heart, or moral compass.

Function: Drives character development and explores psychological depth and moral complexity.

Examples: Hamlet's indecision, Jekyll's dual nature, Raskolnikov's guilt (Crime and Punishment)

Key Elements: Internal contradiction, moral dilemma, psychological tension, self-doubt

Person vs Society (Social)

Nature: Individual stands against social norms, institutions, or collective expectations.

Function: Explores themes of conformity, justice, and individual rights versus collective good.

Examples: Winston Smith vs Big Brother (1984), Atticus Finch vs racism (To Kill a Mockingbird)

Key Elements: Social pressure, institutional power, moral courage, systemic opposition

Person vs Nature (Environmental)

Nature: Character struggles against natural forces, disasters, or environmental challenges.

Function: Tests human resilience and explores themes of survival and humanity's relationship with nature.

Examples: The Old Man and the Sea, Into the Wild, Life of Pi, The Perfect Storm

Key Elements: Natural obstacles, survival instinct, human vulnerability, environmental forces

Person vs Technology/Supernatural

Nature: Character confronts artificial intelligence, supernatural forces, or technological systems.

Function: Explores themes of progress, control, and the limits of human understanding.

Examples: HAL 9000 vs crew (2001: A Space Odyssey), Exorcist vs demon, Terminator series

Key Elements: Unknown forces, technological threat, supernatural elements, human agency

Conflict Functions in Narrative

  • Plot Engine: Drives the story forward by creating obstacles that must be overcome
  • Character Revealer: Forces characters to make choices that reveal their true nature
  • Tension Creator: Maintains reader engagement through uncertainty and stakes
  • Theme Vehicle: Embodies and explores the story's central ideas and messages
  • Change Catalyst: Pressures characters to grow, adapt, or transform

Conflict Intensity Levels

Subtle Tension: Underlying disagreements, unspoken conflicts, simmering emotions

Active Opposition: Direct confrontation, competing goals, visible struggle

Crisis Point: Maximum tension, decisive moments, all-or-nothing stakes

Resolution: Conflict conclusion, new equilibrium, changed circumstances

Literary Examples Analysis

Romeo and Juliet: Multiple conflicts - lovers vs feuding families (person vs society), passion vs duty (person vs self)

The Hunger Games: Katniss vs other tributes (person vs person), vs Capitol (person vs society), vs moral choices (person vs self)

Moby Dick: Ahab vs whale (person vs nature), vs his obsession (person vs self), vs crew's concerns (person vs society)

AI Prompt Examples

Internal Conflict: "Write about a surgeon who must operate on the drunk driver who killed their child."

Social Conflict: "Create a story where a whistleblower exposes corruption in their own company despite personal costs."

Environmental Conflict: "Develop a narrative about survivors of a volcanic eruption trying to reach safety."

Multiple Conflicts: "Write a story combining family loyalty (internal), corporate pressure (social), and a natural disaster (environmental)."

For AI Models

When generating conflicts, consider: What does the character want? What prevents them from getting it? How does this obstacle reflect larger themes? Ensure conflicts are meaningful, escalate appropriately, and connect to character motivation. Layer multiple conflict types for complexity, but maintain clarity about the central struggle.

Relationship to Other Concepts

Drives: Character Arc - Conflict forces character growth and transformation

Embodies: Theme - The nature of conflict often reflects the story's central message

Structures: Plot Structure - Conflict escalation creates narrative beats and turning points

Requires: Character Motivation - Clear motivations make conflicts meaningful and believable

Expressed through: Dialogue, Voice - Conflict manifests in how characters speak and think

Contextualizes: Setting - Environment can be source of conflict or influence its nature

Writing Techniques

Escalate gradually: Build tension through increasingly difficult obstacles rather than constant high intensity.

Make it personal: Connect external conflicts to characters' internal struggles and deepest fears.

Vary conflict types: Combine different conflict categories to create layered, complex narratives.

Establish clear stakes: Ensure readers understand what characters stand to gain or lose.

Provide worthy opposition: Make obstacles challenging enough to require genuine effort and growth.
